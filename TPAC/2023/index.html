A more adaptive web

Who needs a more adaptive web?

  • People with cognitive and learning disabilities, such as dyscalculia, AD(H)D, short-term memory loss, etc.
  • People who are in a time of crisis; have a migraine; are busy
  • People who use Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) (with varying vocabulary sizes)
  • People who struggle to learn/recognise new icons

All of us, at some time or another!

Day-to-day impact

Accessing critical services…

  • Joining meetings at work (on time…)
  • Can I make a doctor's appointment?
  • Can I make a complaint to the water company?
  • Can I turn on the heating?

Standards exist to make these interfaces accessible such as the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)

Where are we going?

Content Usable

How are we supporting increased adaptation?

  1. Working with community organizations such as Blissymbolics, with whom we've published the Alternative and Augmented Communication (AAC) Symbol Registry.

  2. Developing a specification for content authors, where a small addition of machine-readable semantic metadata can support a range of helpful adaptations.. The Symbols Module is a Candidate Recommendation.

  3. Working with other groups to build adaptivity upon existing specs.

Access to web content for symbol users

Augmentative and alternative communication (AAC)

Symbols

User need: Users may need symbols to help them understand the concepts behind the content on the page, using the symbol set they know.

Solution: Allow authors to attribute symbol-set-agnostic concepts to parts of the content on the page.

Symbol markup example

<ul>
  <li><span adapt-symbol="22380">Ingredients</span></li>
  <li><span adapt-symbol="16245">Prepping</span></li>
  <li><span adapt-symbol="22909">Cooking</span></li>
  <li><span adapt-symbol="13690">Plate</span> and taste</li>
</ul>

We are only mapping individual concepts to individual symbols—not translating.

Authoring/Bootstrapping

  • The approach needs input from authors.
  • We aim to make this as easy as possible. Authoring-time tooling support is being investigated.
  • We've seen things like <main> work very well, because there is wide potential applicability/neatness for developers.
  • If we can show the potential gains for users, we can create demand.
  • We're aiming for the sweet spot, where a small addition of machine-readable semantics supports a wide range of helpful adaptations.

Further work and explorations

Further work

This work is currently being explored…

Simplification: Many people find too many on-screen options to be problematic. Extra example: someone with a migraine. Simplification demo

Purpose: Icons, symbols and pictograms help people identify the purpose of interactive elements. There are many different icons and symbols. Purpose, action, and destination demo

Action, Destination: Users need to understand what a button will do, or where a link will take them. Purpose, action, and destination demo

Distraction: Content of various types and purposes can distract users.
